03 · Course frameworks

Use the right tool for the tension you are in.

These are the models embedded in the simulator. Open each panel for the operating logic, failure modes, and practice moves behind the choices.

A

Conflict stylesTimingRisk

Fighter, Negotiator, Diplomat, Avoider, and People Pleaser each solve a different part of conflict. Each one also creates predictable risk when overused.

Practice signalAsk what the moment needs: speed, learning, repair, cooling down, or trust building.

Fighter works when a fast, principled boundary is needed. Negotiator works when both sides have real constraints and hidden assumptions to surface. Diplomat works when temporary alignment matters more than solving everything now.

Avoider can be wise when emotions are too hot or the issue is not worth the relationship cost. People Pleaser can build goodwill, but it becomes risky when agreement hides what the team actually needs to say.

How to choose

Start with stakes and timing. If user harm, ethics, or team safety are on the line, a stronger boundary may be needed. If both sides hold partial truth, negotiate. If the team is flooded, cool the system before solving.

Failure mode

Fighter becomes dominance, Negotiator becomes over-trading, Diplomat becomes vague compromise, Avoider becomes silence, and People Pleaser becomes false agreement.

Practice move

Name your instinct before acting: "My first impulse is to smooth this over. What would the work need if I were not trying to keep everyone comfortable?"

B

AIDSBIRadical Candor

AID moves from Action to Impact to Development. SBI moves from Situation to Behavior to Impact. Radical Candor asks for care personally and challenge directly.

Practice signalSpecificity lowers defensiveness. Care keeps directness from becoming dominance.

Use AID when you want a clear development path. Use SBI when the first job is to ground the conversation in observable behavior. Use Radical Candor when the risk is either false harmony or harsh truth without enough care.

In critique, remember the difference between feedback and critique: critique evaluates the work against the objective; feedback helps a person improve behavior, understanding, or collaboration.

AID

Action names what happened. Impact explains why it matters. Development proposes the next behavior.

SBI

Situation grounds the moment, Behavior stays observable, and Impact names the effect. It avoids labels until shared facts are clear.

Radical Candor

Challenge without care can feel like attack; care without challenge can become unclear kindness. The pairing is the leadership move.

Critique container

Name the objective, the decision needed, the kind of feedback wanted, and the criteria for judging the work.

C

PowerInfluenceNegotiation

French and Raven's sources of power, Lukes' dimensions of power, Cialdini's influence principles, and creative negotiation all show up in lateral work.

Practice signalLook for agenda power: what is being decided, and what is never being discussed?

Referent, expert, informational, connection, and resource power can all help a designer influence across functions. The simulator rewards moves that make that influence visible without turning it into a power struggle.

Creative negotiation asks you to understand who, what, when, where, and why before anchoring a path forward. Integrative negotiation creates shared gain; distributive negotiation treats the moment as a zero-sum contest.

Read power first

Ask who can decide, who can block, who has information, who has social trust, and whose needs are missing from the agenda.

Use power cleanly

Expert power should clarify risk. Informational power should help the group decide. Connection power should bring the right people together.

Negotiate creatively

Separate positions from interests. A PM may protect a date, engineering may protect capacity, and design may protect user confidence.

Anchor with options

Bring two or three paths with explicit tradeoffs: fastest, safest, and recommended. Options make the decision real.

D

ABC triangleListeningRepair

The ABC triangle separates Attitude, Behavior, and Context. It helps you avoid overreacting to the visible behavior while missing the system beneath it.

Practice signalBegin from curiosity and respect. Be direct. Expect a positive outcome.

Attitude includes assumptions, emotions, and perceptions. Behavior includes the visible statements and actions. Context includes the political, cultural, historical, and economic background that shapes the tension.

Strong repair often starts with one sentence that acknowledges impact, one question that reveals context, and one proposal that creates a next experiment.

Attitude

Look for the invisible story: fear of missing a deadline, anxiety about quality, resentment from past decisions, or assumptions about authority.

Behavior

Stay concrete. "The request bypassed intake" is easier to work with than "stakeholders do not respect design."

Context

Context explains why reasonable people may be acting badly: incentives, leadership pressure, unclear ownership, scarce time, or weak process.

Repair script

"I want to pause on what happened, because the impact matters. What constraint were you solving for? Here is what I need us to try next time."

04 · Team rehearsal kit

Turn one scenario into a team learning ritual.

Run this in twenty minutes with a design team, product trio, or leadership peer group. The goal is shared language, not perfect answers.

1. Read

Read the scenario aloud and name likely Attitude, Behavior, and Context signals.

2. Vote

Everyone privately chooses a first move. Compare instincts before revealing coaching.

3. Rewrite

Rewrite the strongest response in your team's actual voice: direct, specific, kind.

4. Transfer

Pick one real meeting this week where the same leadership move could help.
